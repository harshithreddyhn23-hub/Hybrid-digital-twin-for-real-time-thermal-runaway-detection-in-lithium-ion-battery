# Hybrid-digital-twin-for-real-time-thermal-runaway-detection-in-lithium-ion-battery

Hybrid Digital Twin for Real-Time Thermal Runaway Detection in Lithium-Ion Batteries – Detailed Project Explanation

The rapid adoption of lithium-ion batteries in electric vehicles (EVs), renewable energy storage systems, drones, laptops, and portable electronic devices has significantly increased the need for reliable battery safety monitoring systems. Although lithium-ion batteries provide high energy density, long cycle life, and excellent performance, they are susceptible to a dangerous phenomenon known as thermal runaway. Thermal runaway occurs when excessive heat generated inside the battery cannot be dissipated efficiently, causing the battery temperature to rise uncontrollably. This chain reaction can lead to electrolyte decomposition, gas generation, fire, or even explosion. Therefore, early prediction of thermal runaway is one of the most important challenges in modern Battery Management Systems (BMS).

The primary objective of this project is to develop a Hybrid Digital Twin capable of continuously monitoring battery parameters, estimating battery behavior, predicting future battery temperature, assessing battery risk, and providing an early warning before thermal runaway occurs. Instead of depending only on a physics-based battery model or only on an artificial intelligence model, this project combines both approaches to improve prediction accuracy and reliability.

A Digital Twin is a virtual representation of a real physical system. In this project, the lithium-ion battery is represented digitally inside a computer. Normally, in an industrial environment, a Digital Twin receives live sensor data from the battery, including voltage, current, temperature, state of charge (SOC), internal resistance, ambient temperature, and other operating parameters. Since this project is implemented without physical hardware, an existing lithium-ion battery dataset is used as a substitute for live sensor readings. The dataset acts as a virtual sensor stream by replaying each data sample sequentially, allowing the Digital Twin to simulate real-time battery monitoring.

The first stage of the project involves loading the battery dataset into Google Colab using Python. The dataset contains multiple charging parameters such as battery voltage, charging current, average battery temperature, maximum battery temperature, ambient temperature, internal resistance, state of charge, battery health indicators, and event labels indicating different battery operating conditions. Before using the data for prediction, preprocessing is performed by removing unnecessary columns, handling missing values if present, encoding categorical variables, normalizing numerical features, and preparing the data for machine learning. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is then conducted to understand relationships among battery parameters. Various graphs such as temperature trends, voltage variations, charging current plots, correlation heatmaps, and feature distributions are generated to study battery behavior during charging.

The next stage is the construction of the Physics-Based Digital Twin. Unlike conventional machine learning projects, a Digital Twin incorporates physical principles that describe battery behavior. In this project, the battery temperature is estimated using Joule's Law, where heat generated inside the battery is proportional to the square of the charging current multiplied by the internal resistance. The generated heat increases the battery temperature, while heat dissipation to the surrounding environment decreases it. A simplified thermal model estimates the next battery temperature by combining heat generation and cooling effects. This virtual model represents the physical battery inside the computer and serves as one part of the Hybrid Digital Twin.

Although physics-based models explain battery behavior, they cannot capture every nonlinear phenomenon occurring inside the battery. Therefore, an Artificial Intelligence model is introduced. A Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural network is selected because battery charging data is naturally sequential. LSTM networks are specifically designed to learn temporal dependencies from time-series data. Instead of predicting battery temperature using only the current sensor reading, the LSTM receives multiple previous observations of voltage, current, temperature, SOC, ambient temperature, and internal resistance. By analyzing historical battery behavior, the model predicts the future battery temperature more accurately than conventional regression methods.

Once both prediction models are available, the project implements the Hybrid Digital Twin by combining the predictions from the physics model and the LSTM model. Initially, a simple weighted average fusion strategy was considered. However, fixed weights cannot adapt to changing battery operating conditions. To improve the system, an Adaptive Fusion Algorithm is introduced. A battery risk score is calculated using normalized values of battery temperature, charging current, and internal resistance. Under normal operating conditions, the physics-based model receives higher importance because the battery follows expected thermal behavior. As the battery approaches abnormal operating conditions, such as increasing temperature or high charging current, the AI model becomes more dominant because it can recognize complex nonlinear patterns learned from historical data. The adaptive weights continuously change according to the battery risk score, allowing the Hybrid Digital Twin to generate a more reliable temperature prediction.

After obtaining the hybrid temperature prediction, the project evaluates the battery safety level. Based on the calculated risk score, the battery is classified into different operating states such as Normal, Warning, Critical, and Thermal Runaway. This classification provides a clear indication of battery health and enables early intervention before dangerous operating conditions develop. If the battery enters the Warning or Critical region, the system generates an alert. If the battery reaches the Thermal Runaway region, an emergency warning is displayed, indicating that immediate action is required to prevent battery failure.

To simulate a real Battery Management System, the dataset is replayed row by row instead of processing all data simultaneously. Each row represents one incoming sensor measurement, similar to data received from real battery sensors. During simulation, battery temperature, voltage, charging current, hybrid predicted temperature, battery risk score, and battery operating status are continuously updated. Although the project currently uses a historical dataset instead of live sensors, the simulation closely resembles real-time battery monitoring. In future work, the same Digital Twin framework can be connected to physical sensors such as temperature sensors, voltage sensors, current sensors, and an ESP32 microcontroller without changing the core prediction algorithm.

The performance of the proposed system is evaluated using standard regression metrics such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error (MSE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and coefficient of determination (R²) for temperature prediction. If thermal runaway is treated as a classification problem, additional metrics including Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and Confusion Matrix can be calculated. The Hybrid Digital Twin is compared against the Physics-Based Digital Twin and the standalone LSTM model. The expectation is that the hybrid approach produces lower prediction errors because it combines the strengths of both physical modeling and artificial intelligence.

One of the main contributions of this project is the implementation of an adaptive fusion strategy rather than relying on a single prediction model. Traditional battery monitoring systems often depend entirely on physics-based equations or purely data-driven models. In contrast, this work integrates physical battery knowledge with deep learning and dynamically adjusts their contribution according to battery operating conditions. This hybrid strategy improves prediction robustness while maintaining physical interpretability.

Although the project currently operates using an offline dataset, the architecture is fully prepared for real-time deployment. By replacing the dataset with live sensor measurements from a Battery Management System, the same Digital Twin framework can continuously monitor battery behavior in electric vehicles or energy storage systems. Future improvements may include integrating IoT communication protocols such as MQTT, cloud-based Digital Twin synchronization, explainable artificial intelligence techniques, reinforcement learning for adaptive battery control, and hardware validation using ESP32 or Raspberry Pi platforms.

In conclusion, this project demonstrates a complete Hybrid Digital Twin framework for lithium-ion battery safety monitoring. It combines a physics-based thermal model, an LSTM deep learning model, adaptive prediction fusion, real-time simulation, battery risk assessment, and thermal runaway detection into a single intelligent monitoring system. The proposed approach provides a foundation for developing next-generation Battery Management Systems capable of predicting dangerous operating conditions before catastrophic battery failure occurs. With additional experimental validation, stronger baseline comparisons, and hardware integration, this framework has the potential to be extended into a high-quality IEEE research contribution in the field of intelligent battery management and digital twin technology.
